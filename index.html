Introduction

Being twin planet of Earth, Venus is not so familiar due to lack of space exploration. With 93 bar pressure and 450 degree C surface temperature, venus is a synonym for hell. Due to such extreme conditions, the landers which were send to the surface of venus could stand only few hours running. Our major challenge for exploration of venus lies on making reliable energy source that could withstand the extreme conditions. As NASA has planned for Long-Live In-Situ Solar System Explorer for 60 days with minimized cell discharge rate.

SOFC

The most promising solution we found is Low Temperature Solid Oxide Fuel Cell. Fuel Cells are the perfect candidate because in compared to conventional batteries, they offer higher specific energy, scalable, provid mass and volume savings for extended period of operation of landers. [1] . Fuel cell is an electrochemical device which generates electricity by continuous flow of reactants. It is extremely efficient because unlike other electrochemical sources which needs to be converted to thermal, then mechanical and then electrical energy, it directly converts to electrical energy with 1 step process. It is a conversion device which works until fuel gases and oxidants are supplied to electrodes.

SOFC has 3 working parts Cathode, Anode and Electrolyte . Hydrogen gas H2 is used as fuel gas and oxygen as oxidant.
Cathode must be porous and must have electrical conductivity. Electrolyte must be a highly dense solute which has only ionic conductivity for oxide ions. Anode must be porous too and should have mixed conductivity. That means it must conduct electrons as well as oxide ions.
Oxygen is passed to cathode and is reduced to oxide ions (O2-) in 3 phase boundary (Cathode, electrolyte and oxygen). Electrolyte conducts oxide ions towards anode where hydrogen gas is being blown. Then hydrogen and oxide ions react to give water and electrons. Potential difference is created between anode and cathode, thus by connecting a current carrying conductor between anode and cathode, electricity is generated.

Conventional SOFCs have operating temperatures above 800 degrees Celsius, considering Venus's surface temperature, which is quite high. Therefore we need to use a Lower Temperature SOFC that works in the venus surface temperature but lowering temperature results in low ionic conductivity in electrolytes. To make working possible, we are selecting suitable anode, cathode, electrolyte interconnect, and sealant.

Configuration

There are two major ways of cell design configuration.

  1. Planer
  2. Tabular
The cells are designed in the planar configuration because of higher power density(1.8-2 W/cm^2), Lower manufacturing cost(US$800/kW), compact design and lightweight, less energy loss, and better mobility, in comparison to the tubular configuration which has lower power density (0.2-0.3 W/cm^2) and focuses on constraint design. Moreover, the planar configuration can be designed in 3 styles
  1. Electrolyte-supported
  2. Anode-supported
  3. Cathod-supported

Among these Anode supported configuration suits our needsthe best. Cells built with an anode as the support structure have the advantage of having a thin electrolyte membrane. This greatly reduces the ohmic loss attributed to the electrolyte thickness, which enables lower operating temperature and higher performance. The lower operating temperature greatly reduces degradation (related to the corrosion of the ancillary reactant delivery equipment and adverse effects on the cell), thereby increasing lifetime. Additionally, this design has a thin cathode layer that provides an opportunity for tailoring the cathode material formulation to enhance performance or provide chemical resistance for various applications. A downside to anode-supported cells is that the nickel cermet support must be porous to allow for gas diffusion; thus, it is harder to seal the periphery. In addition, the diffusion resistance of the anode gases through this thick anode support structure makes it difficult to achieve high single-pass fuel utilization.
